title: "Strengths and blind spots — triangulated"
---

Josh doesn't guess at his strengths — he triangulates them. Three independent data sources live in his vault: a survey 13 of his friends answered in 2024, a Wharton career assessment from 2026, and a workplace personality instrument. A claim only counts as conviction when multiple sources agree.

The three strengths every source confirms:

1. Comfort with risk. His single highest assessment score (91st percentile), paired with Security ranked dead last among his motivators. Behaviorally proven: he left a stable professional engineering track mid-career for a full-time MBA. He commits before the picture is complete, and it energizes him rather than scaring him.

2. Persuasion and fast trust. Leadership 90, Sociability 89, Confidence 87. Nine of thirteen friends independently described him as the person who energizes and motivates others — one called him "a thermostat, not a thermometer." Combined with technical depth, he can walk into a room of engineers or executives and move them to a decision.

3. Turning complexity into a clear decision. Strategic and critical thinking in the mid-80s; recommenders describe him as someone who "translates technical to strategic." He takes ambiguous, technical situations and produces the structured answer people can act on.

The blind spot he publishes on purpose: listening. In 2024, the survey of 13 friends flagged that he can take over conversations and visibly check out when uninterested. Two years later, the Wharton assessment independently scored Listening and Empathy as his two lowest skills — same finding, different instrument, two years apart. No single document showed this; only connecting them did (see [[Who I Am]] in the vault). He treats it as his primary growth edge: let people reach answers themselves, name what's good in an idea before critiquing it, treat silence as someone forming a thought rather than a gap to fill.

He shares the weakness deliberately: a knowledge system that only stores your highlights is a scrapbook. One that confronts you with your patterns is a mirror.
