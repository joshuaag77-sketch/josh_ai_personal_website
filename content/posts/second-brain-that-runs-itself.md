A while back I wrote about turning my Obsidian vault into a second brain that actually knows me. Every person, idea, and decision, indexed and linked, queryable forever.

It worked. But I noticed something: I was still the only thing that made it move. The vault knew everything and did nothing. It was a library, and libraries wait for you to show up.

I wanted a colleague instead. So I gave it three standing jobs.

## The gardener

The first one runs a couple mornings a week and just tends the place. It pulls anything evergreen out of my messy daily notes and files it properly. It fixes broken links. It flags things that have been sitting in my inbox too long. The unglamorous maintenance I never do myself.

The part that makes it work is memory. It keeps a ledger. When I undo something it did, that becomes a permanent rule — it never suggests that again. It gets more like me every week instead of starting from zero.

## The brief

The second one runs Sunday evenings and writes me a short report. Not a summary of what I already know — the opposite. Its main job is to find a thread I couldn't see myself: two notes I wrote weeks apart, never linked, quietly making the same argument in different words. Then it draws the conclusion neither note reached on its own.

The first one it found connected a note about calling and ambition to a note about my career thesis. Turned out I'd been answering a question in my career logic that I'd claimed, elsewhere, I still hadn't figured out. I hadn't seen it until something read both pages at once.

## The forge

The third one is a bench of ideas worth writing about — like this one. It only lets an idea through if it passes a simple test: could someone without my specific experience have written it? If yes, it's rejected. Generic advice wearing a personal anecdote is still generic advice.

## The actual point

None of these are chatbots I talk to. They're jobs that run whether I show up or not, and everything they produce writes back into the same vault. The gardener keeps it clean, the brief mines it for insight, the forge turns insight into things worth publishing.

The knowledge compounds. But now so does the work done on top of it.

That's the part I didn't expect. A second brain that knows you is useful. A second brain that acts on what it knows — quietly, on a schedule, getting sharper each pass — is a different kind of thing entirely.
